Overview

Background

Originating from 1994, Mirrors is a successful brick and mortar clothing company with over 400 stores across 300 countries.

Details

Role: UX Researcher, UX/UI Designer
Timeline: June 2020 - July 2020
Tools Used: Figma, Illustrator
Skills: End-to-end product design

Design Opportunity

The rise of online shopping has led Mirrors customers to question their lack of a parallel e-commerce experience. Many cited the inconvenience of finding physical items and stock in stores .

Design
Solution

From the research phase, it was clear that an e-commerce website was needed to address both user and business goals.
To address original customer complaints and pain points, a responsive website was created. I tackled the company's business goals by building unique features such as an interactive "new arrivals" catalog page.

Understanding the market and industry standards

Market Research

Before approaching the users, I wanted to understand the market, industry standards, and common frustrations that existing companies were facing.

In fast fashion, companies utilized the consumer-driven mindset. Consumers were always be looking for fresh looks, fast.
If consumers couldn‚Äôt buy or find the item of clothing they were looking for, they would not hesitate to look elsewhere.
With the rise of sustainable manufacturing, people wanted to know the manufacturing details of their product and 46% were willing to pay higher prices for these goods.

Understanding the Users

User Research

Five remote user interviews were conducted via Zoom. Through contextual inquiry and interview questions, I challenged my pre-existing assumptions and had the opportunty to discover a typical user's flow, thought process, and pain points when shopping online.

Insights
  1. Users frequently looking for product reviews
  2. Users are more inclined to use brands they have established trust with
  3. Users have a deeper product consideration when shopping online
  4. Users enjoy the convenience of online shopping
  5. Users seek external validation and support
  6. Users enjoy having access to a larger variety of options
  1. Users enjoy product reviews
    When shopping with a brand, people may initially have an underlying distrust with companies they infrequently use. 3/5 users said they do not trust the company description and photographs, as ‚Äúthey‚Äôre obviously trying to sell me something‚Äù. From here, 5/5 users used reviews as proven testimonies from other successful (or unsuccessful) customer purchases as a factor in their decision. 4/5 users also relied heavily on competitor product and price research to see if they were truly getting their money‚Äôs worth.
  2. Users are more inclined to use brands they have established trust with
    From the interviews, 3/5 users stated that they preferred to use brands they had established a trusting relationship with. This was developed through consistent positive experiences with factors such as customer service, positive communication between company and user, and straight forward return policies. They would also rely less on competitor research and product reviews (point 1). It can be interpreted that the user wants to feel appreciated and respected by the company they have chosen to spend their money with.
  3. Users have deeper product consideration when shopping online
    Due to shipping fees and other hidden costs such as custom and duty fees, online shoppers end up paying more than just the cost of the product on a website. This contributes to why 4/5 users said they either have to do online competitive research or deeply think about an item before purchasing. Furthermore, any waived additional fees or offered discounts/promotions from the company was a motivator for 5/5 of the usersmaking them feel like they‚Äôre getting some value back from the company and that they‚Äôre spending their money wisely. Waived fees would bring the overall product cost down to what the user would pay in store, and they‚Äôd be more inclined to purchase the item. When given free shipping and returns, 2/5 users said that they shopped with less restraint. An example given was where they ordered two different sizes of the same item if they were unsure of the fit.
  4. Users enjoy the convenience with online shopping
    From the interviews, 5/5 users stated that they liked the convenience of online shopping. It saved them time from having to go to the store and finding the item they were looking for. They didn‚Äôt have to leave their home, they could conveniently shop ‚Äúon-the-go‚Äù wherever and whenever, and their product would ship straight to their door.
  5. Users need reassurance and support
    4/5 users stated that when shopping online, they missed having the option of a sales associate to assist them. They would use the sales associate for help finding an item or offering opinions regarding choices. 2/5 users said they ‚Äúmiss the attention‚Äù. This connects with point 2 (brand trust) as a sales associate can contribute to a positive customer service experience and subsequent brand trust.
  6. Users enjoy having options
    5/5 users liked to online shop because they felt they had access to greater variety of options that would not be offered in their local store. Tied with point 6 (convenience of shopping), they also enjoyed being able to not just browse products, but use filters to narrow their search to browse specific categories. The option of having a wish list or ‚Äòsave for later‚Äô function was common amongst 3/5 users, where an item peaked their interest but not enough to purchase yet. They liked having being able to save the item in what they call a ‚Äúsecond cart‚Äù, and have the option to return to it if they changed their mind. A wish list almost plays into the indecisive nature of some individuals, giving them a sense of reassurance (point 5) that they can always return to purchase the item if they change their mind.
Persona

The interviews showed three different archetypes of the typical online shopper.
Olivia Miller was a detailed creation of the 'saavy shopper' archetype (user #1 and #4)- a busy go-getter who shops online at least once per week and prioritizes convenience, and the best price and quality. The insights above were also included in Olivia's persona as they were common across all interviews. By incorporating them, I was able to design for the wider audience that Mirrors ultimately hoped to appeal to.
Due to the time constraints of the project, only this archetype was created.

My assumption was wrong: users had two unique mental models.

Information Architecture

It was important to find out how and where a user would think to look for a product on the site.
A remote card sorting activity with 30 (hypothetical) products was conducted to discover the users‚Äô mental models.

I originally assumed that only traditional category labels (ie. tops/bottoms) would be used. The card sorting results showed otherwise. Instead of one, people actually had two types of categories when grouping products.

  1. Traditional group labels
  2. Traditional group labels

In short, the results showed that the specificity of a product name determined the category users thought they'd be under. By incorporating a mix of traditional category labels and more specific category labels, Mirrors would be able to satisfy both unique mental models.

Learnings

As I write this case study, I look back at this project fondly. During this time, I applied everything I learned and received the full end to end experience as a product designer. The art of fashion was always something I loved to explore, but also learning the business behind the industry was awesome.

  1. During a short period of time, I was exposed to a lot of design patterns in the e-commerce space. I learned to understand the design decisions behind each one, and how to find the relevant patterns and best practices.

  2. Fashion is also a business, people! During the project, I learned the importance of taking the time to establish business and user goals. By taking this moment, I found similarities in both, and that overlap was the foundation for my designs.
