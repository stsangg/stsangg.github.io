Designing for scale
Responsive navigation redesign for the Spelling Bee of Canada

UX Research, Information Architecture, Interaction Design, UI Design

Background

Designing a navigation is like designing a map. When done correctly, people rely on it to orient themselves on a new website and can eventually learn their way around. When done poorly, the navigation becomes less like a map and more like a maze.
Armed wth collected feedback from our community of parents (target users), my goal was to improve the design and experience of our MVP website’s main navigation.

View designs
Problem

In a discovery interview with the client, I learned that our current navigation had 3 high-priority problems:


Frustrating information architecture

Parents reported experiencing difficulty with a direct business goal: competition registration. They were used to the old website design's information architecture, where the link to register was accessible from the menu. In the redesign, the link was a level-lower and located at the bottom of a page. This mismatch in thinking resulted in a lot of frustration and support emails.


Poor interaction design

Simply put, it was hard to see what items were in an active state. When a parent hovered over an item, the only indicator was the switching of the chevron icon. Though there was the dropdown, parents still found it hard to establish a relationship between that and the navigation item with such little affordance.


Considering scalability

The goal was to eventually release more resources and features for parents and competitors, leading to more pages. This was the best time to consider how those features could be seamlessly integrated and organized in the site's information architecture.

Secondary Research

Understanding client expectations

My first step was understanding what the client envisioned and had roadmapped for www.spellingbeeofcanada.ca. Having an idea of our scalability helped me tailor my research towards more complex navigation designs, which eventually led to the discovery of mega menus. Referencing research from the NNGroup, the mega menu showed multiple levels of a site’s information architecture in a single view.
I found that this would help the user by:

  • see and learn the categorical relationships
  • visualize instead of exerting mental effort to try to remember where each action is

There was also value in thoughtfully considering content. A common problem with navigations was the use of words that had little meaning to the user, thus leaving them lost and confused. To combat this, I opted to use more action-oriented terms so parents could arrive on the site and understand what actions they could take under each item.

Card sorting activity

Diving into how the parents think

Because parents were confused with the existing information architecture, I decided to run a card sorting activity. The goal was to discover how they think about and group the list of items in the ideal navigation.

Participants: 9

Duration: 4 days

Analyzing the similarity matrix , I estimated that items grouped together minimum of 55% of the time should be categorized together in the navigation. Items without groupings of 55% of higher were grouped together with the next highest percentage.

Responsive design

Learning from our first launch

More differentiation in the interaction

A blue line was used to show the different states of a navigation item. The blue was already in our color palette, and provided enough contrast against the yellow background (added bonus that it also complementary to the yellow!). The dropdown was also moved to appear below the line, a common design pattern found in my research. The same interaction was applied to the sub-nav items where on hover, the blue line would appear and there was a thickness in font weight.

Juggling UI and UX priorities

Visually, enforcing a maximum of 2-columns in each dropdown would've provided visual balance. However, the "media" category had a better association with "About", a category with 2-columns already. I made the decision to prioritize the information architecture and group "media" under the category it was better associated with.

Incorporating friendliness with icons

Although the spelling bee competitions have an air of professionalism, we wanted the website to also appear inviting and friendly to anybody who landed on it. To break up the text and add a friendly visual element, I added icons in the category heading.

Thoughtful order

Thinking down to the order of the categories, I followed the order shown on other sites and started the navigation with "About" and ended with "Contact". This supported by my secondary research.


But wait, we can’t forget about responsiveness

Unfortunately, there wasn't a good way to bring responsiveness to the mega menu. To find a similar design that could work in tablet and mobile size, I went back to the original purpose of why I even started using the mega menu: to support more navigation items. I began researching how smaller screen designs handled this issue, and discovered the sequential menu.

Similar to the mega menu, the sequential menu only shows the subcategories of the last selected menu. Similar to the mega menu, it established a clear visual hierarchy and relationship between the sub-nav items. The only concern (which will be tested), was that parents would need to click multiple times through different levels to find a specific item.
