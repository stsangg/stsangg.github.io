Background

Coming pre-installed in over 40.8 million iPhones in 2019, people are no stranger to Apple‚Äôs Calendar and Reminders app. Despite being separate things, they share the same goal: reminding people of an upcoming event or item. By integrating and improving task management features from Reminders to the Calendar app, people can conveniently stay organized and on top of their schedule in one place.


Details

Role: UX/UI Designer
Timeline: 4 weeks
Tools Used: Figma
Skills: UX Research, UX/UI Design

Approach
Design Opportunity

Though great for its basic functions, preliminary research showed people thought Apple‚Äôs Calendar and Reminders apps to be inconvenient. They provide limited capability to individuals who prefer a single, more robust system of schedule management. For an improved user experience, I asked if and how the applications could be combined.

Design Solution

The research showed there was a real need for a schedule management system within the Calendar application. I designed new features to motivate people to stay on top of their schedule in an familiarly organized and structured format.

Overview of the day's progress

At a quick glance, know where you are in your day and what you need to do. Following the psychology behind Apple's fitness rings, a productivity tracker helps motivate people to complete their tasks and close their rings.

Simultaneously manage events and tasks

View your already scheduled events when choosing a due date to avoid overbooking. Say goodbye to stress and burnout.

See the day in one place

The calendar view shows both events and scheduled tasks in one place to eliminate the frustration of switching between applications. The task drawer is conveniently collapsible if people need a full calendar view.

What do the competitors offer?

Wireframes

What fits within Apple's design system?

To find a layout and design that would best fit the existing design system, I started with a series of low-fidelity sketches. Building on my ideas, I used Apple's material design kit, to build a Figma prototype for testing. I continued with Apple's branding shade of blue as the highlight color throughout. Given that the nature of the app could easily cause stress or anxiety (see Deadline Dan), the blue would be calming to users.

Low fidelity

Mid fidelity

Calendar integration
People were frustrated with relying on their memory to plan their week around previously scheduled events. When choosing a task due date, people can view the events for the day to avoid overbooking and stress.


My Day
When viewing the full task list, people were getting overwhelmed and demotivated. My Day provides a focused view of the day's events and tasks. Productivity rings visually track the day's progress and motivate people to finish the day and "close" their rings.


Split calendar view
People have different preferences for viewing their schedule. A split screen between the calendar and tasks provide an alternative focused view for the day.


Usability Testing

Testing design flows

With the personas' goals in mind, I conducted a remote concept usability and task-based testing.

Focus on evaluating:

  • providing a one-stop-shop experience for people to plan their schedule
  • offering a basic structure for people with different management methods
  • motivating users to stay on track with their day

Measuring success:

  1. > 70% of users complete the task
  2. < 70% of users are confused
  3. Overall positive feelings towards concept and design

Apple's accessibility problem

When taking inspiration from Apple's fitness UI, I noticed that the brighter colors chosen were not accessible against the classic white background. This was a problem when looking at their Health app, as the activity text did not meet WCAG standards. This is where I strayed from their design kit, and modified my text colours to a darker shades.

Learnings
  1. Creativity isn't linear
    2. Initially, it was hard to come up with a novel solution and not just recreate what was already on the market. The brainstorming sessions helped me because it showed that the best ideas are never just "one idea". By thinking of many different ideas, I was able to piece together different elements of each and "frankenstein" a novel solution.
  2. Always double check (even at big companies)
    3. All designers are human, and this is something I may have forgotten as I often look up to current designers as role models. This project reminded me of this fact, and taught me to never just blindly follow a company's visual UI. Remembering design principles and double checking my work is how I discovered the accessibility issue. I hope to one day be a part of making that company improvement!
